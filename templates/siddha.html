96 Thattuvams (Elements of Siddha)

The 96 Thattuvams describe how the human body and mind function. They include physical structures, sensory organs, intellectual abilities, emotional qualities, and spiritual components. Siddha uses these 96 principles to explain how diseases arise and how healing happens from within.

Understanding these elements helps identify how herbs can influence digestion, energy, behaviour, strength, and overall body balance.

Panchabootham (The Five Elements)

According to Siddha, every human and every herb is made of five natural elements. Healing becomes possible when these elements are in harmony.

  1. Earth (Pirithi) — Structure, bones, stability.
  2. Water (Apas) — Blood, fluids, moisture.
  3. Fire (Agni) — Digestion, heat, metabolism.
  4. Air (Vayu) — Circulation, movement, breathing.
  5. Space (Akasha) — Mind clarity, sound, expansion.

Herbs are chosen based on how they help balance these elements in the body.

How Siddha Is Related to Herbal Medicine

Siddha is deeply rooted in the study and use of herbs. For thousands of years, Siddhars observed plants, trees, roots, minerals, and natural elements to understand their healing effects. Siddha medicine depends on herbs because they carry natural energies that match the body’s inner balance.

In Siddha, each herb is understood not only by its physical properties but by:

  • 🌿 Its taste (Suvai) and how it affects the body
  • 🌿 Its cooling or heating energy (Veeryam)
  • 🌿 Its impact on Vatha, Pitha, and Kapha
  • 🌿 Its ability to strengthen organs and purify the body

Because of this deep connection, a herbal garden becomes the best place to experience Siddha knowledge in a practical and natural environment.

What Is Siddha?

Siddha is one of India’s oldest healing systems. It focuses on balancing the body using herbs, diet, minerals, meditation, and daily routines. Unlike modern medicine which targets symptoms, Siddha aims to restore natural harmony.

Every person carries three vital forces—Vatha (air movement), Pitha (fire/heat), and Kapha (water stability). Illness occurs when these go out of balance, and herbs help restore equilibrium.

Siddha identifies herbs through taste, energy, behaviour, and long-term effects, making it a holistic plant-based healing system.

Siddha View of Plants

1. Suvai (Taste)

Sweet, Sour, Salt, Bitter, Pungent, Astringent — each affects heat, digestion, and strength differently.

2. Veeryam (Potency)

Describes whether a plant warms or cools the body.

3. Pirivu (Post-digestive Effect)

Explains what a herb does after digestion — crucial for long-term effect.

4. Gunam (Qualities)

Light, heavy, oily, dry, soft, sharp — they influence how quickly herbs work.
